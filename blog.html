I Contain Multitudes: The Microbes Within Us and a Grander View of Life
[Biology]
Ed Yong's "I Contain Multitudes" dives into the fascinating world of microbes living within us. From bacteria that help us digest food to viruses that shape our immune system, these tiny organisms play a far bigger role than just causing sickness.

The book explores these complex partnerships between microbes and various creatures, including humans, deconstructing the view that microbes are bad and symbiosis is necessarily good. Here are some interesting facts you'll encounter:
(1) Microbes have been around for so long that all life forms have evolved in their presence, shaping our biology and behavior. Modern day hygiene practices may have unintended consequences by disrupting these ancient relationships, as can be seen in the rise of allergies and autoimmune diseases.
(2) Animals (and everything else) are teeming with microbes that influence our development, immune system, and even behavior. Microbes in cows and termites help them digest food that their bodies can't break down on their own. Some deep-sea dwellers rely entirely on microbes for energy.
(3) Microbes can transfer genes between species, allowing them to adapt to new environments and challenges with extreme speed. This gene swapping can lead to antibiotic resistance in bacteria and the spread of beneficial traits in other organisms by selecting/nurturing those microbes that help them survive.
(4) Microbiome as an ecosystem: The human body is a complex ecosystem where microbes interact with each other and with our cells. Disruptions in this ecosystem may lead to diseases like obesity, diabetes, and autoimmune disorders. Understanding these interactions can help us develop 'new' treatments such as faecal transplants, and be weary of the unintended consequences of antibiotics.
Through these examples, Yong argues that we should see ourselves not as individual organisms, but as complex ecosystems shaped by our microbial companions; truly a grander view of life.

Immune: a Journey into the Mysterious System that Keeps You Alive
[Biology]
"Immune" by Philip Dettmer is a fascinating exploration of the human immune system, its functions, and its impact on our health. Dettmer delves into the complex world of immunology, breaking down concepts in an accessible manner for readers of all backgrounds.

"Immune" takes readers on a journey through the intricate workings of the immune system, from its basic components to its remarkable ability to defend against pathogens and maintain overall health. Dettmer discusses various aspects of immunity, including the roles of white blood cells, antibodies, and cytokines, as well as the processes of inflammation and immunological memory. He also explores the ways in which the immune system can malfunction, leading to autoimmune diseases, allergies, and immunodeficiency disorders. Throughout the book, Dettmer emphasizes the importance of maintaining a balanced immune system through proper nutrition, exercise, and stress management.
(1) Immune Cells: The human body contains several types of immune cells, each with its own specialized functions. These include macrophages, T cells, B cells, and natural killer cells, all of which work together to identify and eliminate threats to the body.
(2) Antibodies: Antibodies, also known as immunoglobulins, are proteins produced by B cells in response to specific antigens, presented via MHC molecules. They play a crucial role in neutralizing pathogens such as bacteria and viruses, preventing them from causing harm to the body.
(3) Immunological Memory: One of the most remarkable aspects of the immune system is its ability to remember past encounters with pathogens. This immunological memory allows the body to mount a faster and more effective response upon subsequent exposure to the same pathogen, providing long-lasting protection against reinfection.
(4) Hygiene Hypothesis: The hygiene hypothesis suggests that the increasing prevalence of autoimmune diseases and allergies in modern society may be partly attributed to reduced exposure to infectious agents during childhood. According to this hypothesis, early exposure to certain microbes helps train the immune system and reduce the risk of developing autoimmune disorders.

Transformer: The Deep Chemistry of Life and Death
[Biology]
In "Transformer: The Deep Chemistry of Life and Death," Nick Lane challenges traditional views on life by placing the spotlight on a fundamental biochemical process: the Krebs cycle / citric acid cycle.

The book argues that a central role in life's origin and function belongs to the Krebs cycle, a series of chemical reactions that can both break down molecules for energy and build them up for growth. Lane proposes that:
(1) Life's Origin at Deep-Sea Vents: The book suggests that early life forms might have emerged near deep-sea hydrothermal vents where the right chemical gradients existed to drive the Krebs cycle in reverse, building organic molecules.
(2) Metabolism Takes Center Stage: Understanding life revolves more around how cells manage energy and transform molecules than just genetic information.
(3) Krebs Cycle And Genes: The ability to harness energy through the Krebs cycle may have existed before the complex machinery of genes and proteins came into play. Moreover, the Krebs cycle's intermediates and mitochondrial DNA can have many complex effects on nuclear gene expression.
(4) Cancer and Aging: Lane proposes that the differences between healthy and cancerous cells lie partly in their metabolic efficiency and the signals metabolic intermediates release, with cancer cells prioritizing rapid growth over efficient energy use, and ageing cells being in an epigenetic setting that corresponds to low metabolic turnover.
Overall, "Transformer" offers a fresh perspective on life, emphasizing the primacy of metabolism and the Krebs cycle in shaping the living world from its origins to the complexities of health and disease.

[Misc]
In Kruiden, Kokkels en Kippen, Dutch scientist and author Louise Fresco explores the scientific journey through the lens of two historical figures.

Georg Rumphius: A 17th-century German naturalist working for the Dutch East India Company (VOC). Despite facing setbacks like earthquakes, blindness, a fire, and even a shipwreck that destroyed his collected specimens, Rumphius's detailed observations and writings on plants and animals in Southeast Asia made him a posthumous scientific celebrity.
Christiaan Eijkman: A late 19th/early 20th-century Dutch physician who, while researching a nerve disease in chickens, accidentally stumbled upon the concept of vitamin deficiencies. This discovery, though initially attributed to his colleague who proposed the underlying theory, later earned Eijkman the Nobel Prize.
Fresco uses their stories to highlight the realities of scientific discovery: Science is rarely a linear path: It often involves hard work, trial and error, and even some luck. Tenacity and perseverance are crucial: Both Rumphius and Eijkman faced challenges but continued their work. Scientific recognition can be fickle: Eijkman's accidental discovery initially overshadowed the theoretical groundwork laid by his colleague. Through these examples, Fresco pays tribute to the dedication of researchers throughout history and emphasizes the ongoing quest to understand the world around us.

The Spike: An Epic Journey Through the Brain in 2.1 Seconds
[Neuroscience]
The Spike: An Epic Journey Through the Brain in 2.1 Seconds by Mark Humphries takes a deep dive into the fundamental unit of communication in the brain: the electrical spike.

Humphries starts with the basics, explaining how neurons communicate with each other through electrical spikes. These spikes are the language of the brain, carrying information from one neuron to another. The book explores how these spikes are generated, how they are transmitted, and how they are decoded by the receiving neuron. The book then delves into some problematic observations that do not correspond to simple input-output spike models. Examples are neuron's abilities to spike at rates that seem not sustainable, or the amount of silent or dark neurons that do not react to external stimuli, or how single or only few stimuli cross the action potential threshold. He theorizes that these silent neurons maintain information in their spike trains and keep the trigger threshold low, ready to fire when needed when few stimuli are presented. This resolves the speed, ease, and continuity at which action potentials can be formed. He interestingly links this to predictive processing and the brain's ability to expect and anticipate future events. The intricate dance between excitatory and inhibitory neurons is another key concept. While excitatory neurons fire to send signals, inhibitory neurons act as a brake, ensuring balanced processing and preventing runaway activity.

Active Inference: The Free Energy Principle in Mind, Brain, and Behavior
[Neuroscience] [Mathematics]
Active Inference: The Free Energy Principle in Mind, Brain, and Behavior by Thomas Parr, Giovanni Pezzulo, and Karl Friston offers a comprehensive and mathematical look at Karl Friston's theoretical framework for understanding cognition and behavior.

Borrowed from physics, the book revolves around the concept of minimizing "free energy." Imagine free energy as a measure of surprise or prediction error. The brain, according to this theory, constantly tries to minimize this surprise by actively engaging with the world through perception and action. Active inference aims to provide a unified explanation for various cognitive phenomena, including perception, attention, memory, and planning. It suggests that all these functions serve the ultimate goal of reducing free energy and maintaining a stable internal state (allo- & homeostasis). The book explores how active inference can explain phenomena like why we pay attention to certain things and ignore others. It suggests that our attention is drawn to things that help us reduce uncertainty and minimize free energy. Active inference is a computational framework. The book dives into specific models that demonstrate how the theory can be used to explain real-world behaviors. These models can potentially be used to develop robots or AI systems that can actively learn and adapt to their environment.

The Extended Selfish Gene
[Biology]
The Extended Selfish Gene by Richard Dawkins is a follow-up to his highly influential book, The Selfish Gene. Dawkins expands on his original view that genes, not individual organisms, are the fundamental unit of natural selection.

Dawkins argues that genes are "replicators" - their primary function is to make copies of themselves and pass them on to future generations. Organisms (or vehicles), in this view, are simply "survival machines" built by genes to ensure their own propagation. Altruistic behaviors, from a gene's perspective, are only beneficial if they ultimately increase the chances of that gene surviving and being replicated. He introduces the concept of memes - ideas or cultural traits that spread through imitation, similar to how genes replicate. This concept has been debated and expanded upon in the field of cultural evolution. The "Extended" concept expands the scope of selection. Dawkins proposes that genes can exert their influence not just on individual organisms but also on their environment and the behavior of other organisms.

[History] [Natural Sciences]
In Jared Diamond's thought-provoking book, "Guns, Germs, and Steel," he tackles a big question: Why did some societies develop complex technologies and empires while others remained hunter-gatherers? Diamond argues that the answer lies not in racial superiority, but in geographical advantages.

The book explores how factors like:
(1) Favorable environments for domestication: Eurasia and Africa had a wider range of plants and animals that could be domesticated for food, leading to settled agriculture and population growth.
(2) Spread of diseases: Animals in close proximity to humans in Eurasia transmitted diseases like smallpox and measles, unintentionally giving these populations some immunity.
(3) Spread of culture: Living on the same latitudes allows societies to grow the same types of crops due to similar climates, leading to the spread of agricultural practices, and spread of technologies and culture as well (e.g. the silk road).
These factors, according to Diamond, created a snowball effect. "Guns, Germs, and Steel" is not without its critics, but it remains a compelling read that forces us to reconsider how our world came to be the way it is.
