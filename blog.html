The Last Drop
[Environmental Science] [Society]
The Last Drop by Tim Smedley explores the global water crisis, arguing that the issue isn't a lack of water on Earth but rather how humans mismanage and distribute it.

Smedley explains that the world's water isn't disappearing, but its cycle is intensifying due to climate change. As the planet heats up, more water evaporates, increasing atmospheric water vapor, which is a greenhouse gas itself. This intensifies the water cycle, leading to more extreme weather events. Wet areas get wetter, and dry areas get drier, causing more frequent and intense storms, floods, and droughts. The book highlights that while these events are natural, human activity is increasing their intensity and frequency.

  • Mismanagement of Water Resources:
    • Over-extraction from aquifers and rivers exceeds the rate at which they can be replenished. Empty aquifers can collapse, leading to structural damage to the land above.
    • Human Engineering like dams, canals, and reservoirs disconnect rivers and lakes from their underground aquifers, disrupting natural water cycles and reducing the availability of groundwater. Even desalination can have significant environmental impacts, such as harming marine ecosystems and increasing greenhouse gas emissions due to high energy costs.
    • Pollution from sewage, agricultural runoff (manure and fertilizers), and pharmaceuticals creates ecological imbalances, such as algal blooms that harm biodiversity. Industrial waste from sources like old mines introduces heavy metals and other harmful substances such as micro- and nanoplastics into water systems.
  • Solutions and Innovations:
    • Agriculture the largest consumer of water, can adopt eco- and water friendly practices like no-till farming to improve soil health and water retention as well as implementing crop rotation and cover cropping.
    • Tiered water pricing can incentivize conservation by charging more for higher usage, encouraging more responsible water use.
    • Rainwater harvesting can be implemented to collect and store rainwater for irrigation and other uses, reducing reliance on groundwater and surface water sources. It is also effective for off-grid systems.
    • Wastewater treatment can be improved to make treated wastewater safe for irrigation and industrial use, reducing the demand for freshwater.
    • Nature-based solutions are central to the book's argument. These include rewilding, restoring natural wetlands, and even reintroducing animals like beavers to create natural dams and water filtration systems. Or, removing water-greedy invasive species that disrupt the local water cycles.
Overall very interesting insights into water management, although it can be heavy and overdone in terms of statistics and policy. I think Tim Smedley captured the problem at its core in the introduction "The world isn't running out of water - people are."

Ondraaglijke Lichtheid
[Philosophy] [Society]
"Ondraaglijke Lichtheid" (Unbearable Lightness) by Ilja Leonard Pfeijffer is a collection of essays that critically examines the pervasive role of irony in contemporary society, particularly in public discourse and social interactions.

Pfeijffer argues that while irony can be a powerful tool for wit and critical thought, its overuse and misuse have led to a superficiality and a decline in genuine engagement, making it difficult to discern sincere meaning and fostering a climate of cynicism.

  • Linguistics:
    • Etymology of Irony Pfeijffer delves into the historical roots of irony, tracing its etymology back to the Greek word "eironeia" (εἰρωνεία), which literally means "simulated ignorance." This original meaning highlights the fundamental characteristic of irony: saying one thing while intending another, often to subtly mock or challenge a perceived truth. Understanding this origin helps to appreciate how the concept has evolved and diversified over centuries.
    • Breaking Language Maxims Pfeijffer explores how irony often involves a deliberate flouting of conversational maxims, such as Grice's maxims of quantity, quality, relation, and manner. By breaking these maxims, speakers can create a layer of meaning that invites listeners to engage in a more nuanced interpretation of the message.
  • Problematics:
    • Identifying Irony Missing information may make it hard to discover flouting of maxims, and thus to identify irony. Pfeijffer discusses how the lack of context or shared knowledge , e.g. written irony or personal/cultural references, can lead to misunderstandings, where listeners may take ironic statements at face value, leading to confusion or misinterpretation.
    • Negatives in Public Debates He argues that when irony becomes the dominant mode of communication, it can lead to a pervasive ambiguity. Speakers can easily retract their stated positions by claiming "it was just irony," making it difficult to hold anyone accountable for their statements. This creates a corrosive environment where genuine arguments are undermined, and sincere conviction is often met with suspicion or derision, leading to a breakdown in constructive dialogue.
    • Trivialization of Serious Topics By consistently treating grave issues with a detached, sardonic, or cynical tone, irony risks diminishing their actual weight and urgency. This can lead to a collective desensitization, where pressing social, political, or ethical concerns are not taken seriously enough, hindering meaningful action and fostering apathy among the public. The constant use of irony can inadvertently signal that nothing truly matters, undermining the very possibility of sincere engagement.
A short but very insightful read on the role of irony in our society, and how it can be both a powerful tool for critique and a dangerous weapon that can undermine genuine discourse and invite apathy. I conclude that irony has a value in fostering critical thinking and challenging dominant narratives, and in smaller kindred circles, but it must be wielded with care to avoid the pitfalls of cynicism and disengagement.

Clearing the Air
[Environmental Science] [Society]
"Clearing the Air" by Tim Smedley is a comprehensive and urgent exploration of the pervasive problem of air pollution, detailing its varied forms, profound health impacts, and the complex web of sources that contribute to it.

Smedley's book serves as a crucial wake-up call, dissecting the invisible threats that permeate the air we breathe. He explains the different types of airborne pollutants, how they interact with our bodies, and the devastating consequences for human health across all ages. Beyond identifying the problems, "Clearing the Air" meticulously uncovers the diverse origins of these pollutants, from vehicle emissions and industrial activities to agricultural practices and household products.

  • Pollutants:
    • Greenhouse Gases These gases trap heat in the atmosphere, contributing to global warming and climate change. Smedley discusses how they are primarily produced by burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial processes. While not directly harmful to human health, their role in climate change has far-reaching consequences for ecosystems and human societies.
    • Nitrogen Oxides Nitrogen oxides (NOx) are a group of gases that are produced from burning fossil fuels at high temperatures, such as in vehicles and power plants. They contribute to the formation of ground-level ozone, which can cause respiratory problems and other health issues.
    • Particulate Matter These are tiny particles (e.g. PM2.5 and PM10, micrometer scale) that can penetrate deep into the lungs and bloodstream, causing respiratory and cardiovascular diseases, and even impacting brain health, as well as carry other harmful substances like heavy metals and organic compounds. The smaller they are the more surface there is to have harmful substances and the further they can penetrate tissues.
  • Sources:
    • Vehicle Emissions Cars, trucks, and buses are significant sources of air pollution, releasing pollutants like NOx and particulate matter into the atmosphere, and more importantly, directly near people's lives.
    • Industrial Activities Factories and power plants emit a variety of pollutants, including greenhouse gases and particulate matter, often as a byproduct of burning fossil fuels.
    • Agricultural Practices Agriculture contributes to air pollution through the release of ammonia from livestock waste and fertilizers, as well as methane from enteric fermentation in ruminants.
  • Regulations & Evasion:
    • Dieselgate Volkswagen's emissions scandal, where the company used software to cheat on diesel emissions tests, highlights the need for stricter regulations and enforcement in the automotive industry. Smedley discusses how this incident exposed the industry's willingness to prioritize profits over public health and environmental protection.
    • Shifting the Problem As regulations push for stricter emissions standards, some industries may relocate to areas with looser regulations, effectively shifting the pollution burden geographically or towards finer PM or different pollutants, rather than eliminating it.
    • Wins Smedley also highlights successful initiatives and policies that have led to improvements in air quality, from China to the United States where measurements and strict policy have shifted the tides. The discussions on policy often circle back to the tension between economic growth and environmental protection.
One interesting aspect of Smedley's book is that he, throughout his travels, takes air quality measures, and they are often surprising in a negative sense. He finds that even in seemingly pristine environments, air pollution can be a hidden threat, affecting both human health and the environment.

Er is leven na de groei
[Society]
"Er is leven na de groei" by Paul Schenderling argues for a post-growth society in The Netherlands, challenging the relentless pursuit of economic expansion as the sole measure of progress and well-being.

Schenderling, an economist, contends that continuous growth is not only unsustainable on a finite planet but also increasingly detrimental to human flourishing. It reminds me of "comfort is not the same as well-being". He proposes a shift towards a society that prioritizes ecological balance, social equity, and genuine quality of life over mere material accumulation.

  • Consumerism:
    • Planned Obsolescence This is the deliberate design of products to have a limited lifespan, encouraging consumers to replace them frequently. Schenderling argues that this practice, widespread in modern economies, artificially inflates demand and accelerates resource depletion, directly conflicting with post-growth principles.
    • Jevon's Paradox This states that as technological efficiency in resource use increases, the consumption of that resource often increases as well, rather than decreasing. For example, more fuel-efficient cars might lead to more driving. This paradox undermines the idea that technological fixes alone can solve environmental problems without a fundamental shift away from growth-oriented thinking.
    • Degradation & Pollution This refers to the environmental harm caused by excessive consumption and waste. Schenderling highlights how the pursuit of growth often leads to the over-extraction of resources, habitat destruction, and increased pollution, all of which threaten the planet's ecological balance.
    • Externalities These are the indirect costs of consumption that are not reflected in market prices, such as environmental degradation and social inequality. Schenderling argues that a post-growth society must address these externalities by implementing policies that internalize these costs, promoting a more equitable and sustainable economic system.
  • Solutions:
    • Current Measures Current measures are like carbon taxes, subsidies for renewable energy, and regulations on emissions fail to address the root causes of overconsumption and environmental degradation. Schenderling argues that these siloed measures often focus on reducing greenhouse gas emissions and move the problem to some other form of environmental or societal impact.
    • The Proposed Measure Schenderling proposes a regressive tax on consumption (not just on production), which would disproportionately affect higher-income individuals who tend to consume more. This tax aims to reduce overall consumption levels, encourage more sustainable practices, and generate revenue for social programs that support those most affected by the transition to a post-growth economy.
    • Supporting Measures One main one is to reduce the tax on labor, making work more financially rewarding. Other measures include promoting circular economy practices, investing in renewable energy, and enhancing basic services.
  • Outcomes:
    • More Justice (Social & Ecological) A post-growth society would prioritize social equity and ecological sustainability, addressing the injustices of the current system. Schenderling argues that by reducing consumption and focusing on well-being, we can create a fairer society that respects planetary (and human) boundaries.
    • More Connectedness In a post-growth society, individuals may prioritize relationships and community over material possessions. This shift could lead to stronger social bonds, increased collaboration, and a greater sense of belonging.
    • More Happiness By focusing on well-being rather than consumption, people may experience greater life satisfaction. Schenderling argues that a post-growth society can foster environments that support mental health, work-life balance, and personal fulfillment.
Overall Schenderling's book has an optimistic, perhaps even utopian, vision for a post-growth society. He acknowledges the challenges ahead but believes that with the right policies and a shift in values, we can create a more just and sustainable world. I would have loved to read more concrete ideas on the taxation of consumption, perhaps on the incorporation of externalities in market prices.

De Tech Coup
[Society]
In "De Tech Coup," Marietje Schaake, a former Member of European Parliament and current Stanford policy expert, critically examines the increasing power and influence of major tech companies and how their largely unregulated dominance poses significant risks to democracy, privacy, and societal well-being.

Technologies have often outpaced the ability of governments to regulate them effectively. Schaake argues that this unchecked growth has led to a "tech coup," where these companies wield more influence than many governments, shaping public discourse, privacy norms, and even democratic processes. Not all technologies and tech companies are inherently bad, but the lack of regulation and oversight has allowed for abuses of power, as well as the fact that they are not democratically elected representatives wielding this power and control.

  • The Promise:
    • Openness and Transparency: The potential for greater access to information and public discourse. E.g. the internet and social media were initially (and still are) seen and promoted as platforms for free expression and democratization of information.
    • Innovation: The belief that technological advancements would lead to societal progress, economic growth, and improved quality of life. The wonders that AI and other technologies could bring, such as improved healthcare, education, and communication.
    • Decentralization: The promise of distributed power and widespread economic growth.
  • Reality:
    • The Partial Fulfillment: Aside from the critical issues of privacy and security, the promise of innovation has been fulfilled in many ways, with significant advancements have and will continue to improve our lives. E.g., alphafold has revolutionized the field of protein folding, leading to breakthroughs in drug discovery and understanding diseases.
    • Knowledge Centralization: Information and crucial knowledge are concentrated within a few companies, not publicly accessible due to IP rights and company secrecy. This makes it hard for policy makers to keep up with the rapid pace of technological change, and to understand the implications of these technologies on society, and to develop policy on unknowns.
    • Undermining Democracy: Tech CEO's are not elected representatives, yet they wield significant power over public discourse and decision-making processes. This raises concerns about accountability, transparency, and the potential for abuse of power. Misinformation and targeted online campaigns can sway public opinions and affect elections. Furthermore, their influence and lobbying efforts can undermine democratic processes, as they often prioritize profit over public interest.
    • Misuse and Abuse: Autocratic regimes (can) exploit these technologies, and the hacking of well-intended tools can still lead to harmful applications. Marginalized groups often find themselves disempowered or under-represented in big-data rather than empowered. E.g., Apple has removed apps that were used by protestors in China, and the Chinese government has used AI to surveil and oppress its citizens. Similar examples exist for most tech companies, with the goal of not losing out on a huge market.
  • The Now and the Future:
    • Rigorous Regulation: Other sectors have strict regulations. No car would enter the market if it had not been rigorously been tested for safety and environmental impact. Why not some modern technologies? E.g., the pharmaceutical industry has "clinical trials".
    • "Regulation Kills Innovation" Fallacy: Tech companies innovate fast and would like to do so freely, without regulations. However, regulation does not stop innovation, it guides it. Take Formula 1, strict regulations have spurred innovation within new constraints, not stifled it. as well as improved on safety and environmental impact. Taking the speed of unchecked innovation over improved safety and environmental impact is not a trade-off I would go for.
    • International Cooperation and Independent Oversight: Emphasize the crucial role of global collaboration in regulating tech, and ensure independent oversight of third-party financed institutes to prevent undue industry influence. These companies are global, and so should the regulations be. The EU has been a leader in this regard, but more countries should follow suit.
I want to repeat two points Schaake makes: first, that the promise of technology is not inherently bad, but that the lack of regulation and oversight has allowed for abuses of power and control, and the mismatch of public interests and profit. Second, regulation already exists for many sectors, regulatory frameworks can be extended/adapted, and regulation does not stifle innovation, but rather guides it towards public interest. Schaake's book is a call-to-action for policymakers, technologists, and citizens to recognize the need for a balanced approach that harnesses the benefits of technology while safeguarding democratic values and individual rights.

The Future of Energy
[Energy][Society]
Richard Black's The Future of Energy presents a concise and optimistic roadmap for transitioning to a clean energy future. Drawing on his experience as a former BBC environment correspondent and director at the clean energy think tank Ember, Black argues that the world can nearly eliminate fossil fuel use without compromising modern lifestyles.

He identifies five key technologies essential for this transformation. Black emphasizes that these technologies are not only available but are also becoming more affordable due to economies of scale and innovation. He notes that the main barriers to their widespread adoption are political, often influenced by fossil fuel interests. By embracing these technologies, societies can achieve cleaner air, more jobs, and greater energy independence.

  • Five Pillars:
    • Renewable Energy (RE) Generation Primarily wind and solar power, which are becoming increasingly cost-effective.
    • Energy Storage Solutions Advanced batteries that store electricity efficiently, to mitigate the availability of RE.
    • Electric Vehicles (EVs) Replacing internal combustion engines to reduce oil dependence, possibly with the use of the car battery as a storage solution for the grid.
    • Heat Pumps Efficient systems for heating and cooling buildings.
    • Green Hydrogen Produced via electrolysis, suitable for applications where electrification is challenging.
  • Obstacles:
    • Geopolitics Petrostates are reluctant to relinquish influence, seeking to maintain their strategic and economic power in a decarbonized world.
    • Corporate Malfeasance Fossil fuel companies have shifted from outright climate denial to spreading misinformation and delay tactics to preserve market share.
    • Policy Inertia: Governments often move too slowly, hindered by lobbying, short-term electoral cycles, or fear of economic disruption during transition or energy uncertainty.
    • Full Implementation: Some of the five pillars are lacking behind in implementation, while they really complement each other. E.g. the full potential of heat pumps is only realized when combined with renewable energy sources and energy storage solutions, as they require a stable and clean electricity supply to operate efficiently.
Ultimately, The Future of Energy is not a utopian manifesto but a practical blueprint. It shows that the tools for change are already in our hands — the key is to remove the obstacles that prevent their full deployment.

Feeling & Knowing
[Neuroscience]
In "Feeling & Knowing: Making Minds Conscious," Antonio Damasio presents a compelling argument for consciousness as an evolved biological phenomenon, not a sudden emergence. He meticulously traces its origins, highlighting feelings as crucial precursors to consciousness.

Whereas the content has much overlap with his previous book I read, this book is far more accessible and less dense, making it a more enjoyable read. I will highlight only a few points as there is a lot of overlap with Damasio's other works.

  • Precursors to Consciousness:
    • Sensing: Damasio argues that sensing is a fundamental (mostly chemical), rudimentary form of awareness, even preceding feelings. He suggests that anesthetics work by disrupting these basic sensing mechanisms, impacting even organisms like plants and bacteria, hinting at a shared, ancient biological root for awareness.
    • Minds: The development of minds from nervous systems, which are capable of creating maps and images of the external world and the body's internal states, is another key precursor. These mental representations allow for more sophisticated processing of information and the generation of feelings.
    • Perspective: The emergence of a singular, integrated perspective—a coherent "self" that experiences these feelings and mental maps—is crucial for what we recognize as consciousness. This perspective allows an organism to understand its own relationship to the environment.
  • Forms of Intelligence:
    • Non-Explicit: This form of intelligence encompasses chemical, automatic, unconscious processes like homeostatic regulation, reflexes, and implicit learning. It guides most of an organism's behavior without conscious awareness or deliberation.
    • Explicit: Explicit intelligence involves a conscious mind, reasoning, and the ability to deliberately plan and make decisions. Consciousness, in Damasio's view, represents the pinnacle of explicit intelligence, enabling a richer and more detailed experience of the self and the surrounding world.
Crucially, Damasio suggests that most vertebrates and "social" insects are likely conscious. He bases this on the shared evolutionary heritage of what he terms "the old world" (the viscera and its continuous signaling to the brain) and fundamental brain structures like the brainstem. These commonalities, he argues, provide the necessary biological architecture for the generation of feelings and, consequently, consciousness, indicating that a broader range of life forms experience the world in a way we might recognize as conscious. Damasio thus offers a deeply biological and evolutionary perspective on how minds become conscious, extending the realm of conscious experience far beyond humans.

The Strange Order of Things
[Neuroscience]
"The Strange Order of Things" by Antonio Damasio proposes a radical rethinking of consciousness, arguing that feelings are the foundational layer upon which minds, consciousness, and culture are built.

Damasio posits that consciousness didn't suddenly appear with complex brains but rather evolved gradually from basic homeostatic mechanisms. He argues that the earliest forms of "mind" were feelings.

  • The Origin of Feelings:
    • Homeostasis The fundamental state of life regulation, maintaining stable internal conditions, through genetic programs and chemistry.
    • Nerves From the original nerve nets in simple organisms like hydras, to the dense network of nerves around your gut, and the evolution from ancient to modern nervous systems—these are the biological substrates for sensing and regulating the body.
    • Affect The raw, non-conscious physiological changes that occur in the body in response to stimuli, which are the precursors to feelings. These are the immediate bodily reactions that then get translated into a subjective experience.
    • Feelings The first form of consciousness, arising when the brain creates a subjective mental representation of the body's homeostatic state and its physiological changes.
    • Recursive Emotive Loop As nervous systems became more advanced, the brain began responding not only to external stimuli, but also to its own affective outputs. For example, an initial reaction to danger (like increased heart rate or muscle tension) can be sensed again and interpreted as stress or anxiety—a second-order feeling. This loop—feeling a reaction, reacting to that feeling, and then feeling that reaction again—allowed the emergence of increasingly layered emotional experiences such as guilt, anticipation, embarrassment, and existential dread. These recursive emotional states represent the beginnings of emotional self-awareness, where the organism not only reacts to the world but also reacts to itself.
  • Sensing and Learning:
    • Interoception Visceral/chemical sensing, providing information about the body's internal state (e.g., hunger, pain, comfort).
    • Proprioception Musculoskeletal sensing, offering awareness of the body's position and movement in space, but also e.g. direction of the eyes through the eyeball socket, that might function as metadata to create the "I" perspective of subjectivity.
    • Exteroception Sensory portals (vision, hearing, smell, taste, touch) that provide information about the external environment.
    • Correlation The continuous correlation between interoception (internal bodily states) and proprioception/exteroception (external world and body in space) allows organisms to constantly measure and correlate the state of life regulation with the surroundings. This feedback loop is vital for learning and refining adaptive behaviors, ensuring survival and well-being.
  • Consciousness and Cultures:
    • Subjectivity The unique, first-person "what it's like" experience that emerges from the brain's integrated representations of interoceptive and exteroceptive data, with proprioception acting as a sort of "metadata" providing context for the body's interaction with the world.
    • Feeling Feelings For feelings to have an impact and drive behavior, they must be "felt" or consciously experienced by the organism. This conscious awareness of our internal states is what allows them to affect our decisions, learning, and overall life regulation, and drive creative intelligence and cultures.
    • Cultures Consciousness, driven by the imperative to regulate life and optimize feelings, leads to the creation of complex cultures—our collective efforts to improve well-being, manage suffering, and create a more stable and flourishing existence.
Overall an incredibly interesting book, but written in a very dense and academic style (sometimes too much for my taste). Damasio's exploration of the evolution of consciousness and the role of feelings in shaping our minds and cultures is a thought-provoking journey that challenges traditional views of consciousness as a mere byproduct of complex brain activity. He emphasizes the importance of understanding our emotional lives as a foundation for navigating the complexities of existence, and how this understanding can lead to a more compassionate and interconnected world.

The Story of the Human Body
[Biology]
Lieberman meticulously traces the evolutionary journey of the human body, highlighting key adaptations like bipedalism, changes in diet and brain size, and fat storage. He then contrasts this deep history with the relatively recent and dramatic shifts brought about by cultural evolution, particularly agriculture and industrialization.

This rapid environmental change has created a "mismatch" where our bodies are ill-suited to the demands and excesses of modern life. Furthermore, Lieberman introduces the concept of "dysvolution," where cultural and technological solutions often mask the symptoms of this mismatch without addressing the root causes, perpetuating the problem.


"We frequently mistake comfort for well-being."
  • Evolutionary History:
    • Bidepalism & Endurance The evolution of bipedalism he argues comes from having to venture further foor food due to historical climate change 6 million years ago. This not only freed our hands but also transformed our ability to walk and run long distances, making us exceptional endurance athletes. This adaptation was crucial for hunting, scavenging, and migration, shaping our metabolism and musculoskeletal structure.
    • Teeth, Jaws & Brains Changes in our diet, first weaning off fruits and eating less desirable foods, then the consumption of cooked food, led to smaller teeth and jaws, freeing up energy for the development of larger brains. We basically outsourced part of food processing to tools and cooking. This evolutionary trade-off fueled our cognitive abilities and complex social structures.
    • Fat The ability to store fat and how it interacts with our metabolism was a crucial adaptation for survival during periods of food scarcity, providing a vital energy reserve.
  • Cultural Evolution:
    • Agriculture The shift to agriculture provided a more stable food supply but also led to a less various diet, particularly in a rise in carbohydrates and a decrease in fiber and proteins, as well as the increasing population density and interactions with cattle which both amount to never before seen hygiene issues.
    • Industry The Industrial Revolution brought about unprecedented technological progress but also led to more sedentary lifestyles, exposure to new pollutants (air/water quality but also light and noise), and excessive food processing, further exacerbating the mismatch between our evolved hunther-gatherer biology and our modern industrial environment.
  • Mistmatch & Dysvolution:
    • Too much A primary mismatch is the overabundance of readily available, energy-dense food, far exceeding our evolved need and capacity for energy expenditure. This surplus leads to a cascade of metabolic disorders like obesity, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular diseases. Dysvolution is evident in our reliance on labor-saving devices (e.g. cars and elevators) and the reduced need for physical exertion.
    • Too little Conversely, our modern lives often involve too little of certain ancestral exposures. Reduced physical activity leads to musculoskeletal disorders. Lack and varying times of natural light and excessive artificial light contribute to rising rates of circadian disruption and myopia. Reduced exposure to diverse microbes may underlie the increase in autoimmune diseases. Dysvolution is seen in the necessity of glasses to correct vision and the reliance on labor-saving tools that diminish physical demands.
    • Too new Our bodies are also poorly adapted to entirely novel aspects of our modern environment. Ultra-processed foods, with their high levels of refined sugars, fats, and artificial ingredients, contribute to metabolic dysfunction. Prolonged sitting in chairs and the structure of modern shoes can lead to musculoskeletal problems. Dysvolution is exemplified by overly supportive footwear that weakens the intrinsic muscles of our feet.
Overall, Lieberman's book is a well-structured and incredibly convincing account of the modern state of human health and the evolutionary forces that have shaped our bodies. He emphasizes the importance of understanding our evolutionary history to navigate the challenges of modern life and improve our health. The book serves as a call to action, urging us to embrace preventive medicine and make conscious choices that align with our biological needs.

The Nocturnal Brain
[Neuroscience]
"The Nocturnal Brain" by Guy Leschziner explores the often-bizarre and sometimes frightening world of sleep disorders. As a leading neurologist specializing in sleep medicine, Leschziner uses compelling case studies from his own practice to illuminate the complex neurological processes that govern our nights and what happens when they go awry.

The book delves into a range of sleep disorders, moving beyond simple insomnia to explore conditions like narcolepsy, sleepwalking, REM sleep behavior disorder (where sleepers act out their dreams), sleep paralysis, and various parasomnias. Through vivid patient stories, Leschziner unravels the underlying neurological mechanisms behind these conditions, highlighting the intricate relationship between the sleeping and waking brain. He emphasizes that sleep is not merely a passive state of rest but an active and crucial period for brain function.

  • Sleep as an Active Brain State:
    • Sleep Cycles During sleep, the brain cycles through different stages, including REM (Rapid Eye Movement) and non-REM or deep sleep, associated with different functions and brain activity levels.
    • Making Memories Sleep plays a critical role in memory consolidation, where experiences and information from the day are processed and stored for long-term recall. Furthermore, the brain also engages in synaptic pruning during sleep, selectively strengthening important neural connections while eliminating less relevant ones to optimize brain function.
    • Waste Clearance The brain utilizes a sophisticated waste removal system known as the glymphatic system, which is significantly more active during sleep. This system clears out metabolic byproducts and potentially harmful proteins that accumulate in the brain during wakefulness, contributing to overall brain health and potentially preventing neurodegenerative diseases.
  • Sleep Components:
    • Nighttime Amnesia We typically retain little to no conscious memory of our dreams or activities during sleep disorders like sleepwalking. This nighttime amnesia suggests a functional disconnection between the brain regions involved in generating these experiences and those responsible for encoding memories during sleep. The lack of recall highlights the altered state of consciousness during sleep.
    • Inhibiting Movement A crucial aspect of healthy sleep, particularly REM sleep where vivid dreaming most frequently occurs, is the active inhibition of movement by the brainstem. This brain stem inhibition prevents us from physically acting out our dreams, protecting ourselves and our bed partners from potential harm.
    • Dreaming The experience of dreaming is characterized by hallucinogenic activity in the brain, where we perceive vivid sensory experiences, often bizarre and illogical, in the absence of external stimuli. This internally generated reality highlights the brain's capacity for creating complex and immersive worlds during sleep, even without input from our senses. The neurobiological basis of these dream states continues to be an area of active research.
  • Sleep Disorders & Enigmas:
    • Sleepwalking and Sleep Paralysis Conditions like sleepwalking, involving complex motor behaviors during non-REM sleep with impaired consciousness, and sleep paralysis, characterized by temporary inability to move while being conscious during the transition to or from sleep, illustrate opposite disruptions in the inhibition of movement and levels of consciousness.
    • Narcolepsy and Cataplexy Narcolepsy, marked by excessive daytime sleepiness and sudden sleep attacks, often accompanied by cataplexy - a sudden loss of muscle tone triggered by strong emotions - reveals a dysfunction in the brain's regulation of sleep and wakefulness, particularly involving the neurotransmitter hypocretin. These conditions underscore the specific neurochemical pathways vital for maintaining stable states of alertness and muscle control.
    • Lucid Dreaming Lucid dreaming, where individuals become aware of their dreams and can exert some control over them, is a fascinating phenomenon that highlights the brain's ability to navigate between different states of consciousness. This state of awareness during sleep suggests a unique interplay between the brain regions responsible for self-awareness and those involved in dream generation.
    • Overlap with Neurological Conditions "The Nocturnal Brain" emphasizes the significant overlap between various sleep disorders and other neurological conditions, such as Restless Legs Syndrome and Parkinson's disease, and the shared pathways and medications. This interconnectedness suggests that sleep disturbances can be early symptoms or contributing factors to broader neurological issues, highlighting the importance of considering sleep health in the context of overall neurological well-being.
Similarly to the books and descriptions by neurologist Oliver Sacks, Leschziner's writing is engaging and accessible, making complex neurological concepts understandable to a general audience. He weaves together personal anecdotes, scientific insights, and patient stories to create a compelling narrative that sheds light on the mysteries of sleep and its disorders. The book serves as a reminder of the importance of sleep for our overall well-being and the intricate workings of the brain during this enigmatic state.

In Praise of Walking
[Biology]

How Life Works by Philip Ball is an incredibly interesting book of the principles and mechanisms underlying the complexity of living systems by moving past the "it's all genetics" mindset.     • Waddington's Landscapevisualizes cell differentiation as a ball rolling down a landscape with branching valleys. The ball's path is influenced by the cell's genetic and environmental context, leading to specialization into different cell types.

Another example I liked is that bone cells form when they are next to a rigid material, like bone, creating a simple rule to not grow bone where there is none.

[History] [Society]
In Jared Diamond's thought-provoking book, "Guns, Germs, and Steel," he tackles a big question: Why did some societies develop complex technologies and empires while others remained hunter-gatherers? Diamond argues that the answer lies not in racial superiority, but in geographical advantages.

The book explores how factors like:
(1) Favorable environments for domestication: Eurasia and Africa had a wider range of plants and animals that could be domesticated for food, leading to settled agriculture and population growth.
(2) Spread of diseases: Animals in close proximity to humans in Eurasia transmitted diseases like smallpox and measles, unintentionally giving these populations some immunity.
(3) Spread of culture: Living on the same latitudes allows societies to grow the same types of crops due to similar climates, leading to the spread of agricultural practices, and spread of technologies and culture as well (e.g. the silk road).
These factors, according to Diamond, created a snowball effect. Settled societies with abundant food could support larger populations, leading to technological advancements, political structures, and ultimately, the development of guns, germs (diseases), and steel – the tools for conquest. "Guns, Germs, and Steel" is not without its critics, but it remains a compelling read that forces us to reconsider how our world came to be the way it is.

Papyrus reminds us that books are more than just paper and ink. They're vessels of knowledge, testaments to human ingenuity, and sparks that ignite our imaginations.
